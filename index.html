What is a hypotrochoid?

Picture a small wheel of radius r rolling around the inside of a larger fixed ring of radius R. A pen is attached to the wheel, at distance d from its center. As the wheel rolls, the pen traces a curve. That curve is called a hypotrochoid.

Mathematicians call this whole family of shapes a roulette: the general name for any curve traced by a point on one shape as it rolls along another. This app only draws the rolling inside case. Rolling a wheel around the outside of a circle instead produces a related curve called an epitrochoid, which this app doesn't draw.

The four numbers that define the shape

  • R: the radius of the fixed outer ring. This app keeps R = 1 for every pattern. Only its size relative to the wheel matters.
  • r: the radius of the rolling wheel. This is the Wheel radius control.
  • d: the distance from the wheel's center to the pen. This is the Pen offset control.
  • θ (theta): the angle, in radians, that the wheel's center has swept around the ring so far. Pressing Play advances θ over time. It starts at 0 and grows until the pattern is complete.

The equations

At any angle θ, the pen sits at:

x(θ) = (R − r) cos θ + d cos((R − r) · θ / r )
y(θ) = (R − r) sin θ − d sin((R − r) · θ / r )

Each equation has two parts. The first part, (R − r) cos θ or (R − r) sin θ, gives the position of the wheel's center as it travels around the ring. The second part adds the pen's position relative to that center. The pen doesn't just sit still on the wheel: it spins around the wheel's center at rate (R − r) / r, which is faster than the wheel's center moves around the ring whenever r is smaller than R.

This spin rate isn't a free choice. It comes directly from the wheel rolling without slipping: the distance the wheel rolls along the inside of the ring must exactly match the distance that unwinds around the wheel's own rim. Matching those two distances is what produces the (R − r) / r factor.

When does the pattern close?

Write the wheel radius as a fraction in lowest terms: r = m/n, where m and n share no common factor. For example, r = 0.25 is 1/4, so m = 1 and n = 4. This fraction tells you everything about how the pattern finishes:

  • The wheel's center must travel all the way around the ring m times before the curve closes.
  • The finished pattern has n fold rotational symmetry: it looks the same after turning it by one nth of a full circle.

This follows from a general closing condition for hypotrochoids: θ must sweep 2π · lcm(r, R) / R radians for the curve to close, where lcm is the least common multiple, extended to work with fractions. Since R = 1 here, this simplifies to:

θ_max = 2π · m,    where r = m/n in lowest terms

Two examples. r = 0.25 = 1/4 gives m = 1, n = 4: the pattern closes after one loop around the ring and has 4 fold symmetry. r = 0.4 = 2/5 gives m = 2, n = 5: it takes two loops to close, and the result has 5 fold symmetry.

This app rounds r to the nearest 0.01 before working out m and n. That keeps the value it draws with and the value it uses to decide when to stop in exact agreement, so a pattern always comes to a clean, closed stop instead of trailing off.

Special cases

  • d = 0. The pen sits exactly at the wheel's center, so it simply traces a plain circle of radius R − r. With no offset, there's nothing to spin, so no petals form.
  • d = r. The pen sits exactly at the rim of the wheel. This special case is called a hypocycloid, and the curve meets the outer ring at n sharp points, called cusps, instead of rounded petals. The 4 cusped astroid, from r = 0.25, is the best known example.
  • r = 0.5 (so R = 2r). This case is special on its own. If d is not equal to r, the pattern is a perfect ellipse. If d does equal r, the two cusps collapse into a straight line segment. This line-producing case is known as a Tusi couple, named after the 13th century astronomer Nasir al-Din al-Tusi, who used it to model circular motion producing straight-line motion.
  • d greater than r. The pen extends past the wheel's rim, producing looping, self-crossing patterns instead of smooth petals. This app keeps the pen offset at or inside the wheel's edge, so this case falls outside the range you can draw here.

A bit of history

The drawing toy that popularized these curves, Spirograph, was invented by the English engineer Denys Fisher. He built early prototypes from Meccano pieces between 1962 and 1964, first exhibited the toy at the Nuremberg International Toy Fair in 1965, and it reached the US market through Kenner the following year. The underlying mathematics is far older. In 1827, the architect and engineer Peter Hubert Desvignes built a device called the Speiragraph for drawing related roulette patterns, long before this mathematics was turned into a children's toy.
